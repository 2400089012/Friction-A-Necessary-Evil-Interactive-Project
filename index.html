Four domains of friction
Physics
μ ranges: 0.01 – 1.2
Where friction was first defined. F = μN governs everything from car brakes to walking. Without static friction, you couldn't take a single step. Without kinetic friction, everything would slide forever — a world of pure chaos.
UX Design
Intentional resistance
Designers remove friction to create smooth flows — but intentional friction prevents mistakes. A "Are you sure?" dialog is friction that saves you. No friction = accidental purchases, impulsive deletions, regrettable sends.
Economics
Transaction costs
Market friction — taxes, information asymmetry, switching costs — is what allows monopolies to exist but also what makes local markets possible. Zero-friction markets are frictionless dystopias where only giants survive.
Psychology
Cognitive resistance
Psychological friction — the discomfort of change, the effort of thinking — slows bad decisions. Behavioral economics uses friction as a nudge: making junk food harder to grab, making gym sign-ups easier, making 401k opt-out by default.
The friction paradox
01 No tires grip the road without friction. The very thing that wears your tires is what stops you from dying. Both
02 Social friction causes conflict. But it also prevents groupthink. Disagreement is the friction of democracy. Necessary
03 Platform frictionlessness enabled viral misinformation. One-click share = no time to think. Evil
04 Friction generates heat. In engines, this is waste. In muscles, it's warmth. Context determines everything. Both
05 Bureaucratic friction protects citizens from impulsive governance — and also kills innovation. Same force, dual face. Both

Adam — adaptive friction per parameter

All three optimizers start from the same point. Adam adapts friction per parameter — rough terrain gets more resistance, smooth terrain gets less. This is why Adam dominates modern ML.

↓
Friction as enemy

Vanishing gradients

Each layer multiplies the gradient by a small number (sigmoid derivative ≤ 0.25). Across 20 layers: 0.25²⁰ ≈ 10⁻¹². The early layers receive effectively zero signal and can't learn. ReLU activations and residual connections (ResNets) are friction-reducers that solved this.

∿
Friction as enemy

Saddle points & flat plateaus

In high-dimensional spaces, saddle points are far more common than local minima. The gradient is near zero in all directions — the optimizer stalls. Flat plateaus have the same effect: tiny gradients, slow or no progress regardless of learning rate.

λ
Friction as tool

L2 regularization (weight decay)

Adds a penalty proportional to weight magnitude: L_total = L_task + λΣw². During backprop, this adds a drag term −λw to every update. Large weights are pulled back harder. Result: smoother, more generalizable models that resist overfitting.

p
Friction as tool

Dropout

Randomly zero-masks neurons at rate p during training. Forces the network to develop multiple redundant paths — no single neuron can coast. It's surface roughness applied to the computation graph, preventing any one path from becoming a frictionless highway.

τ
Adaptive friction

Gradient clipping

Caps gradient norm to a threshold τ. When gradients explode at loss cliffs or sharp curvature, this acts as a hard speed limiter. Essential for RNNs and transformers on long sequences. Friction only when the terrain demands it.
